What we checked

  • Website safety settings (Security Headers): rules a browser follows to keep users safe from common attacks.
  • Encryption (TLS/HTTPS): the modern “lock” used when people visit your site.
  • Open doors (Ports): which network doors are available from the internet.
  • Addresses (DNS & Email): where your site lives and whether email is protected from spoofing.
  • Other site addresses (Subdomains): other places your brand lives, like api.example.com.
  • Broken signposts (Takeovers): places that point to providers with no content yet (can be abused).

What to fix first

  1. Add a Permissions‑Policy to limit powerful browser features (camera, mic, etc.).
  2. Tighten your Content‑Security‑Policy (CSP) by avoiding wildcards and “unsafe‑inline”.
  3. Improve HSTS by adding includeSubDomains and preload if it fits your rollout plan.
  4. Move DMARC to “reject” once confident, so spoofed emails are blocked instead of just flagged.
  5. Review any “takeover” flags; remove unused DNS entries or publish content with the provider.
Why this matters: These changes reduce phishing risk, prevent common website attacks, and improve user trust.
